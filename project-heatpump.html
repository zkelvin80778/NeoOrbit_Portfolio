1. Executive Overview

Problem Statement

Sustained, crewed exploration of Mars requires energy systems that can operate under reduced solar irradiance, frequent dust accumulation, and limited opportunities for resupply. Photovoltaic systems scale primarily through increased surface area and become constrained by deployment complexity under these conditions, while the availability and production rates of suitable radioisotopes limit radioisotope thermoelectric [cite_start]generators[cite: 12, 13].

The resulting challenge is to develop a scalable surface energy architecture that can operate under [cite_start]reduced and variable solar input without relying on extensive photovoltaic arrays or scarce nuclear materials[cite: 14].

Proposed Solution

This project proposes a solar concentrator architecture that mitigates Martian surface energy constraints by converting diffuse solar irradiance into thermal energy before electrical utilization. A segmented array of parabolic mirrors arranged in a hexagonal geometry focuses sunlight onto a centralized interface that serves both as a heat exchanger for a water-based heat pump system and as a mounting surface for thermoelectric [cite_start]generator modules[cite: 20, 21].

Key Benefit: By decoupling optical collection from electrical conversion and maintaining alignment [cite_start]through active sun tracking, the system prioritizes precision and scalability over large photovoltaic surface area[cite: 22].

2. Design Motivation & Constraints

Energy Density Limits

On Mars, solar power is reduced by heliocentric distance and degraded by dust. Photovoltaic systems struggle with non-uniform illumination. [cite_start]This motivates approaches that increase usable energy density without massive footprints[cite: 24, 25, 26].

Square-Root Law

Under concentration, PV current increases linearly while voltage increases logarithmically, leading to diminishing returns. [cite_start]Thermal loading becomes a dominant issue, restricting high-flux PV scaling[cite: 28, 29].

Thermal vs. Electrical

Thermal pathways tolerate high flux and uneven light better than direct electrical conversion. [cite_start]Absorbing light as heat allows for redistribution and buffering, reducing sensitivity to alignment errors[cite: 32, 33].

4. Optical Design & Fabrication

Mirror Selection

Parabolic mirrors were selected to collimate sunlight into a predictable focal region. [cite_start]A 250 mm hexagonal edge length was chosen to balance manufacturability, stiffness, and handling[cite: 51, 52].

Iterative Prototyping

The design evolved through three major iterations to solve manufacturing defects:

Iteration 1: Used hot glue for assembly. [cite_start]Failure: Created protrusions that distorted the mirror surface[cite: 71, 72].
Iteration 2: Printed flat to save weight. [cite_start]Failure: Warping occurred across unsupported bridge regions[cite: 78, 79].
Iteration 3: Modular design with separated reflective surfaces. [cite_start]Result: Better optical quality, though fragile aluminum foil remains a limitation[cite: 81, 176].

5. Tracking & Alignment

The system uses a "Light Dependent Resistor" (LDR) array to track the sun. Four sensors are arranged in a quadrant. [cite_start]When the system is aligned, all four sensors report equal light intensity[cite: 91, 93].

The control objective is not absolute sun position, but the minimization of differential light intensity. [cite_start]This allows the system to self-correct using simple servo motors[cite: 96].
